10-minute chart checklist

This checklist is designed to quickly review charts before publishing.

Run through this checklist before publishing a chart

How to use this: mark each item as either achieved or not yet. Use it for your own charts or when reviewing someone else’s.

Progress
0 achieved · 0 not yet

Start by reviewing each part of the chart.

Top

Headlines and framing
0/4 achieved

The title is short, concise, and clear at a glance.

A reader should understand the chart’s main point quickly without decoding a long headline.

The title is written as a full sentence in sentence case.

Chart titles should communicate a complete idea, not just a topic label.

The country or region is included in the title or subtitle without duplication.

Make the geography explicit once so the chart is easy to place and not repetitive.

The title reflects the data directly without speculating beyond what is shown.

Keep the wording evidence-based and avoid interpretation the chart itself cannot support.

Middle

Design, readability, and audience clarity
0/4 achieved

The Ember colour palette is applied consistently and energy sources use the correct colours.

Brand consistency helps charts feel credible and comparable across outputs.

The chart is easy to understand without extra explanation.

A good chart should stand on its own for someone seeing it for the first time.

Colour and contrast are used deliberately to guide attention to the main message.

Use emphasis sparingly so viewers know where to look first.

The chart is understandable to a broad audience and prioritises simplicity over decoration.

Accessibility and clarity usually improve charts for specialists as well.

Bottom

Sources, methodology, and caveats
0/4 achieved

The chart includes a source and all underlying data sources are linked.

Do not rely on “Ember analysis” alone where underlying sources should also be credited.

Sources and footnotes are accurate, brief, and do not crowd the chart.

Keep notes useful but tight so they support the chart rather than overpower it.

Any full methodology is properly linked using the report or methodology title.

Readers should be able to trace how the chart was produced when needed.

Important caveats, uncertainties, or data gaps are explained briefly.

A short note builds trust when there are limitations the reader should know.
